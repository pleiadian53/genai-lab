2. The Problem: Adapting Large Pretrained Models

Foundation models — large neural networks pretrained on massive corpora — learn rich, general-purpose representations. To use them for a specific downstream task, we must adapt those representations. The most direct approach, full fine-tuning, updates every parameter:

$$\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \; \mathcal{L}(\text{model}(\mathbf{x}; \theta), \mathbf{y})$$

This works well when you have enough data and compute. But for a model with hundreds of millions (or billions) of parameters, full fine-tuning has serious practical drawbacks:

ProblemConsequence
Memory: must store gradients and optimizer state for all parametersA 7B-parameter model needs ~56 GB in fp32 just for AdamW states
Storage: each task requires a full copy of the model10 tasks = 10 model copies on disk
Overfitting: updating many parameters with few labelsCatastrophic forgetting of pretrained knowledge
Deployment: serving multiple task-specific modelsGPU memory scales linearly with number of tasks

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) addresses all four problems by freezing $\theta_0$ and introducing a small set of trainable parameters $\phi$:

$$\phi^* = \arg\min_\phi \; \mathcal{L}(\text{model}(\mathbf{x}; \theta_0, \phi), \mathbf{y}), \qquad |\phi| \ll |\theta_0|$$

The key insight: we don't need to move all parameters — a small, strategically placed perturbation is enough to steer the model's behaviour toward a new task.

The central trade-off: fewer trainable parameters means less risk of overfitting and cheaper training, but also less capacity to reshape the model's representations. The "sweet spot" depends on how different your downstream task is from the pretraining objective and how much labelled data you have.

4. Soft Prompting (Prompt Tuning)

Core idea

Prepend $M$ learnable vectors $\mathbf{p}_1, \dots, \mathbf{p}_M \in \mathbb{R}^D$ to the input token embeddings. These "virtual tokens" live in the same embedding space as the real input and are processed by the frozen model alongside the input sequence.

The prompted input

Given input token embeddings $\mathbf{Z} = [\mathbf{z}_1, \dots, \mathbf{z}_N] \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ from the frozen embedding layer, the prompted input is:

$$\mathbf{Z}_{\text{prompted}} = [\underbrace{\mathbf{p}_1, \dots, \mathbf{p}_M}_{\text{learnable prompts}} \;|\; \underbrace{\mathbf{z}_1, \dots, \mathbf{z}_N}_{\text{frozen input embeddings}}] \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{(M + N) \times D}$$

Gradient flow

During backpropagation, the frozen backbone acts as a fixed differentiable function. Gradients flow through the backbone but only update the prompt embeddings $\mathbf{P}$ and the classification head:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{P}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \text{output}} \cdot \frac{\partial \text{backbone}(\mathbf{Z}_{\text{prompted}}; \theta_0)}{\partial \mathbf{Z}_{\text{prompted}}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{Z}_{\text{prompted}}}{\partial \mathbf{P}}$$

The backbone's Jacobian $\frac{\partial \text{backbone}}{\partial \mathbf{Z}_{\text{prompted}}}$ is computed but $\theta_0$ receives no gradient update (via requires_grad=False). The computational cost is roughly a full forward + backward pass through the backbone, which is the same as full fine-tuning — the savings come from optimizer memory (only $\mathbf{P}$ and the head need optimizer states) and from storage (one copy of $\theta_0$ serves all tasks).

Parameter count

For $M = 8$ prompt tokens and $D = 128$:

$$|\phi_{\text{prompt}}| = M \times D = 8 \times 128 = 1{,}024 \text{ parameters}$$

Plus the classification head (LayerNorm + Linear): $2D + D \times K + K$. For $K = 2$: $256 + 256 + 2 = 514$. Total trainable: 1,538 parameters out of ~700K (0.22%).

Initialisation matters

Prompt vectors are typically initialised from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ with small $\sigma$ (e.g., 0.02). Alternative strategies include initialising from the model's existing token embeddings for tokens related to the task (e.g., "classify", "promoter"). Lester et al. (2021) showed that initialisation from class label embeddings gives a warm start but the gap shrinks with model scale.

Why "soft" prompting? — In contrast to hard (discrete) prompting, where you hand-craft input text like "Classify this sequence as promoter or non-promoter:", soft prompting learns continuous vectors in embedding space. These vectors are not constrained to correspond to any real token in the vocabulary — they are free to land anywhere in $\mathbb{R}^D$.

5. Deep Prompting (Prefix-Tuning)

Limitation of input-layer prompts

Input-layer soft prompts inject signal at a single point: before layer 1. In a deep model with $L$ layers, the prompt signal may attenuate as it propagates, especially through non-linear operations and normalisation layers. By the time it reaches layer $L$, the prompt's influence may be negligible.

The fix: inject at every layer

Deep prompting (Li & Liang, 2021) maintains a separate bank of prompt embeddings for each layer:

$$\mathbf{P} = \{\mathbf{P}^{(1)}, \mathbf{P}^{(2)}, \dots, \mathbf{P}^{(L)}\}, \qquad \mathbf{P}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times D}$$

At each layer $l$, the layer-specific prompts are prepended to the hidden states:

$$\mathbf{H}^{(l)}_{\text{prompted}} = [\mathbf{P}^{(l)} \;|\; \mathbf{H}^{(l)}]$$

where $\mathbf{H}^{(l)}$ is the sequence of hidden states entering layer $l$.

Reparameterisation for stability

Directly optimising $M \times D \times L$ independent prompt parameters can be unstable. Li & Liang (2021) introduced a reparameterisation MLP: a small feed-forward network that generates the per-layer prompts from a shared lower-dimensional embedding:

$$\mathbf{P}^{(l)} = \text{MLP}(\mathbf{E}^{(l)}), \qquad \mathbf{E}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times d_{\text{reparam}}}$$

The MLP acts as a regulariser — it constrains the space of possible per-layer prompts, preventing them from overfitting. After training, the MLP can be discarded and only the generated $\mathbf{P}^{(l)}$ values are kept.

Parameter count comparison

With $M = 8$, $D = 128$, $L$ layers:

VariantPrompt ParametersFormula
Input-layer soft prompting1,024$M \times D$
Deep prompting (direct)$1{,}024 \times L$$M \times D \times L$
Deep prompting (reparameterised)$M \times d_{\text{reparam}} \times L + |\text{MLP}|$Smaller, more stable
Implementation note for HyenaDNA: Deep prompting in the Transformer sense requires hooking into each layer's forward pass to prepend/strip prompt tokens. For non-Transformer architectures like HyenaDNA's Hyena backbone (which uses convolution-based sequence mixing rather than attention), this requires model-specific engineering. The companion notebook implements a simplified version using the reparameterisation MLP but injects only at the input layer.

6. LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation

Core idea

Instead of injecting new tokens, LoRA modifies the weight matrices themselves — but in a parameter-efficient way. The key observation (Hu et al., 2021): the weight updates during fine-tuning tend to be low-rank. That is, the matrix $\Delta W = W_{\text{fine-tuned}} - W_{\text{pretrained}}$ can be well-approximated by a rank-$r$ factorisation.

LoRA decomposition

For a frozen weight matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\text{out}} \times D_{\text{in}}}$, LoRA learns two small matrices:

$$W' = W_0 + \frac{\alpha}{r} \Delta W = W_0 + \frac{\alpha}{r} BA$$

where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\text{out}} \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times D_{\text{in}}}$, with $r \ll \min(D_{\text{out}}, D_{\text{in}})$.

Forward pass

The modified forward pass for a linear layer $\mathbf{h} = W_0 \mathbf{x}$ becomes:

$$\mathbf{h} = W_0 \mathbf{x} + \frac{\alpha}{r} BA\mathbf{x}$$

The first term uses the frozen weights (computed as usual). The second term is a small parallel computation: project $\mathbf{x}$ down to $\mathbb{R}^r$ via $A$, then back up to $\mathbb{R}^{D_{\text{out}}}$ via $B$. 

              ┌─────────────────┐
              │  W₀  (frozen)    │ ─── main path ──────────┐
  x ─────────►│  D_in × D_out   │                         │
  (D_in)      └─────────────────┘                         │
       │                                                   ▼
       │      ┌───────┐    ┌───────┐               ┌──────────┐
       └─────►│ A     │───►│ B     │──► (α/r)·BAx ►│   +      │──► h
              │ r×D_in│    │D_out×r│               │ (D_out)  │
              └───────┘    └───────┘               └──────────┘
              trainable    trainable

Initialisation

$A$ is initialised from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ and $B$ is initialised to zero. This ensures $\Delta W = BA = 0$ at the start of training — the model begins from the exact pretrained weights. This is a critical design choice: no random perturbation of a well-trained model at initialisation.

Where to apply LoRA

Typically applied to the attention projection matrices $W_Q$ and $W_V$ (and optionally $W_K$ and $W_O$) at every layer. Can also be applied to MLP layers, though this is less standard.

Parameter count

For rank $r = 8$, hidden dim $D = 128$, applied to $W_Q$ and $W_V$ across $L$ layers:

$$|\phi_{\text{LoRA}}| = 2 \times (D \times r + r \times D) \times L = 2 \times 2rD \times L = 4rDL$$

With $r = 8$, $D = 128$, $L = 4$: $4 \times 8 \times 128 \times 4 = 16{,}384$ parameters.

Key advantage over soft prompting: LoRA modifies the model's weight matrices directly, so it can reshape representations at every layer without increasing the effective sequence length. Soft prompting adds $M$ tokens to every forward pass, increasing compute for attention-based models (attention is $O((M+N)^2)$). LoRA adds a small parallel computation at each layer but doesn't change the sequence length.

Merge at inference

After training, the LoRA matrices can be merged into the base weights: $W_{\text{deployed}} = W_0 + \frac{\alpha}{r}BA$. This means zero inference overhead — the adapted model has the exact same architecture and latency as the original.

7. Adapter Layers

Core idea

Adapter layers (Houlsby et al., 2019) insert small bottleneck MLPs inside each transformer block. Unlike LoRA, which modifies existing weight matrices, adapters add entirely new modules.

Adapter architecture

Each adapter is a residual bottleneck:

$$\text{Adapter}(\mathbf{h}) = \mathbf{h} + f(W_{\text{up}} \cdot \sigma(W_{\text{down}} \cdot \mathbf{h}))$$

where $W_{\text{down}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{adapter}} \times D}$ projects down to the bottleneck, $\sigma$ is a nonlinearity (e.g., ReLU or GELU), and $W_{\text{up}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d_{\text{adapter}}}$ projects back up.

Placement

In the standard Houlsby configuration, two adapters are inserted per transformer block: one after multi-head attention, one after the MLP. Each has its own independent weights. 

Standard Transformer Block          With Adapters
─────────────────────────           ────────────────────────
  LN → MHSA → residual               LN → MHSA → Adapter₁ → residual
  LN → MLP  → residual               LN → MLP  → Adapter₂ → residual

Parameter count

Per adapter: $D \times d_{\text{adapter}} + d_{\text{adapter}} \times D = 2Dd_{\text{adapter}}$ (plus biases). With 2 adapters per layer across $L$ layers:

$$|\phi_{\text{adapter}}| = 2 \times 2Dd_{\text{adapter}} \times L = 4Dd_{\text{adapter}}L$$

With $D = 128$, $d_{\text{adapter}} = 32$, $L = 4$: $4 \times 128 \times 32 \times 4 = 65{,}536$ parameters.

Inference overhead: Unlike LoRA, adapters cannot be merged into the base weights. They are additional serial computation at every layer, adding latency. This makes them less attractive for latency-sensitive deployment.

A unifying view: all PEFT methods as residual perturbations

Every PEFT method can be understood as adding a small, task-specific perturbation to the frozen model's computation:

$$\text{output}_{\text{adapted}} = f_{\theta_0}(\mathbf{x}) + \underbrace{\Delta(\mathbf{x}; \phi)}_{\text{task-specific perturbation}}$$

For soft prompting, $\Delta$ enters by changing the input: $f_{\theta_0}([\mathbf{P}; \mathbf{x}]) - f_{\theta_0}(\mathbf{x})$. For LoRA, $\Delta$ modifies the weight matrices: the $\frac{\alpha}{r}BA\mathbf{x}$ terms at each layer. For adapters, $\Delta$ adds residual corrections at each layer. The mechanism differs, but the intent is the same: a lightweight, input-dependent correction on top of a frozen feature extractor.

9. Connection to the ViT CLS Token

In the ViT tutorial, we implemented a learnable [CLS] token — a single vector $\mathbf{z}_{\text{cls}} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ prepended to the patch sequence and trained end-to-end with the full model. Soft prompting generalises this mechanism:

PropertyCLS Token (ViT)Soft Prompts
What1 learnable vector$M$ learnable vectors
WherePrepended to patch sequencePrepended to input embeddings
BackboneTrained end-to-endFrozen
PurposeAggregation point for classificationTask-specific steering of frozen model
Use caseTraining from scratchAdapting a pretrained model
Gradient destinationCLS token + all backbone weightsOnly prompt tokens + head

The conceptual leap: the CLS token is a passive aggregator — it collects information from the sequence through attention. Soft prompts are active conditioners — they inject task-specific context that modifies how the frozen model processes the input. When the backbone is frozen, prompts must work harder because they're the only channel through which task information enters the computation.

Design pattern: "Prepend a learnable vector to a sequence and let the model figure out what to do with it" is one of the most reusable ideas in modern deep learning. It appears in CLS tokens, soft prompting, register tokens (Darcet et al., 2023), and retrieval-augmented generation (where retrieved chunks are prepended to the context).

When to prefer which: The head-only approach excels when you want to combine evidence from multiple foundation models — each model's embeddings are cached independently and the head learns to fuse them. Soft prompting excels when you want to maximally extract from a single model by steering its internal representations. They are complementary strategies, and a hybrid is possible: use soft-prompted embeddings as features for a downstream meta-learner.

Rules of thumb

ScenarioRecommended MethodWhy
Backbone features are already good for your taskHead-onlyCheapest; embeddings are cacheable; no risk of disturbing good representations
Very few labels, opaque backbone (no layer access)Soft promptingMinimal parameters; only needs embedding layer access; backbone-agnostic
Moderate data, need strong adaptationLoRABest balance of efficiency and performance; mergeable at inference

A common pitfall: Soft prompting's performance scales with model size. Lester et al. (2021) showed that for T5-Small, prompt tuning underperforms full fine-tuning significantly. But at T5-XXL (11B parameters), prompt tuning nearly matches full fine-tuning. For small models like HyenaDNA-tiny (700K params), don't expect prompts to close the gap — the model doesn't have enough capacity for the prompts to steer meaningfully. Treat the notebook as a pedagogical exercise, not a benchmark.
